# Causal ML for Decision-Making

**A reproducible research framework for heterogeneous treatment effect estimation, causal graph discovery, and offline policy learning under observational confounding.**

[![Python 3.10+](https://img.shields.io/badge/python-3.10%2B-blue.svg)](https://www.python.org/downloads/)
[![License: MIT](https://img.shields.io/badge/License-MIT-green.svg)](https://opensource.org/licenses/MIT)
[![arXiv](https://img.shields.io/badge/arXiv-2004.14497-b31b1b.svg)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.14497)

Causal inference from observational data is one of the central challenges in modern machine learning: we seek to estimate what *would have happened* under an alternative treatment assignment, armed only with data generated by a possibly confounded behavioral policy. This repository implements a complete pipeline for heterogeneous treatment effect (HTE) estimation—spanning five meta-learners (S, T, X, R, Causal Forest), doubly robust and TMLE-based estimators, continuous DAG structure learning via NOTEARS and PCMCI, and off-policy evaluation using IPS/SNIPS/DR estimators. All algorithms are benchmarked on controlled synthetic data-generating processes with tunable overlap, confounding, and effect heterogeneity, as well as on the semi-synthetic IHDP, LaLonde Jobs, and Twins datasets. Every experiment is reproducible from a single YAML config file, and ground-truth counterfactuals enable exact PEHE, AUUC, and Qini coefficient computation.

## Research Background & Motivation

The fundamental problem of causal inference—we never observe both potential outcomes for the same individual—forces every causal estimator to navigate a bias-variance landscape shaped by confounding, positivity violations, and model misspecification. The Rubin Causal Model (RCM) formalizes this via potential outcomes $Y(1)$ and $Y(0)$, but identification requires strong assumptions (unconfoundedness, overlap, SUTVA) that are routinely violated in practice. When these assumptions break, even sophisticated doubly robust estimators can exhibit surprising failure modes, motivating the controlled benchmarking framework implemented here.

Modern heterogeneous treatment effect estimation goes far beyond the average treatment effect (ATE). In precision medicine, algorithmic hiring, and ad targeting, decision-makers need the **Conditional Average Treatment Effect** (CATE): the expected benefit of treatment for an individual with covariates $x$. The meta-learner paradigm—S-learner, T-learner, X-learner, R-learner, and honest causal forests—offers a modular toolkit where any supervised learner can serve as the base model, but their relative performance varies dramatically with the data-generating process. This repository provides the infrastructure to systematically study these failure modes.

Concurrently, **causal structure learning** from observational data has been revolutionized by continuous-optimization methods that replace combinatorial search over DAG space with a smooth acyclicity constraint. NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018) introduced the trace-exponential characterization $h(W) = \text{tr}(e^{W \odot W}) - d = 0$, enabling gradient-based optimization of the weighted adjacency matrix. For time-series data, the PCMCI algorithm (Runge et al., 2019) combines PC-algorithm skeleton discovery with Momentary Conditional Independence testing to handle autocorrelation and lagged effects.

Finally, **offline policy learning** translates CATE estimates into treatment assignment rules and evaluates them using inverse propensity scoring (IPS), self-normalized IPS (SNIPS), and doubly robust (DR) estimators. These counterfactual policy value estimates allow practitioners to compare targeting strategies without running new experiments—critical in settings where experimentation is expensive or ethically constrained.

The following papers form the intellectual foundation of this codebase:

1. **Rubin, D. B.** "Estimating Causal Effects of Treatments in Randomized and Nonrandomized Studies." *Journal of Educational Psychology* 66(5), 1974. Introduced the potential outcomes framework and the fundamental problem of causal inference that motivates all estimators in this repository.

2. **Künzel, S. R., Sekhon, J. S., Bickel, P. J., & Yu, B.** "Metalearners for Estimating Heterogeneous Treatment Effects using Machine Learning." *PNAS* 116(10), 2019. Formalized the S/T/X-learner taxonomy and established theoretical conditions under which each dominates—directly implemented in `meta_learners.py`.

3. **Nie, X. & Wager, S.** "Quasi-Oracle Estimation of Heterogeneous Treatment Effects." *Biometrika* 108(2), 2021. Introduced the R-learner and Robinson decomposition that decouples nuisance estimation from treatment effect estimation, providing the theoretical basis for the `r_learner` function.

4. **Kennedy, E. H.** "Towards Optimal Doubly Robust Estimation of Heterogeneous Causal Effects." *arXiv:2004.14497*, 2022. Established minimax-optimal rates for DR-learner CATE estimation and the influence-function-based pseudo-outcome that the `dr_learner` implements.

5. **Zheng, X., Aragam, B., Ravikumar, P., & Xing, E. P.** "DAGs with NO TEARS: Continuous Optimization for Structure Learning." *NeurIPS*, 2018. [arXiv:1803.01422](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.01422). Proposed the continuous acyclicity constraint $h(W) = \text{tr}(e^{W \odot W}) - d$ and augmented Lagrangian solver implemented in `notears.py`.

6. **Runge, J., Bathiany, S., Bollt, E., et al.** "Inferring causation from time series in Earth system sciences." *Nature Communications* 10, 2019; and Runge, J. "Detecting and quantifying causal associations in large nonlinear time series datasets." *Science Advances* 5(11), 2019. The PCMCI algorithm combining PC skeleton + MCI tests, implemented in `pcmci.py`.

## Mathematical Foundations

### Potential Outcomes and the CATE

Let $(X_i, T_i, Y_i)_{i=1}^n$ denote observed covariates, binary treatment assignment, and outcome. Under the Rubin Causal Model, each unit possesses two potential outcomes $Y_i(1)$ and $Y_i(0)$, and the observed outcome satisfies the consistency relation $Y_i = T_i Y_i(1) + (1-T_i) Y_i(0)$.

The **Conditional Average Treatment Effect** (CATE) is defined as:

$$\tau(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y(1) - Y(0) \mid X = x]$$

where $\tau(x)$ represents the expected treatment benefit for an individual with covariate profile $x$. The goal of all meta-learners in this repository is to estimate $\tau(x)$ from observational data where $T$ may depend on $X$.

### Identification Assumptions

Identification of $\tau(x)$ from observational data requires three core assumptions:

**1. Unconfoundedness (Strong Ignorability):**

$$\{Y(0), Y(1)\} \perp\!\!\!\perp T \mid X$$

Conditional on observed covariates $X$, treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes. This is the "no unmeasured confounders" assumption—untestable from data alone, motivating the sensitivity analysis module.

**2. Overlap (Positivity):**

$$0 < e(x) < 1 \quad \text{for all } x \text{ in the support of } X$$

where $e(x) = P(T=1 \mid X=x)$ is the propensity score. Every unit must have a non-degenerate probability of receiving either treatment. The `trim_propensity` function enforces this by removing units with $e(x) \notin [0.1, 0.9]$.

**3. SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption):**

$$Y_i = Y_i(T_i) \quad \text{(no interference between units)}$$

Each unit's outcome depends only on its own treatment assignment, ruling out spillover effects.

Under these assumptions, the CATE is identified as:

$$\tau(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X=x, T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X=x, T=0] = \mu_1(x) - \mu_0(x)$$

where $\mu_a(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X=x, T=a]$ is the conditional outcome mean under treatment arm $a$.

### AIPW Pseudo-Outcome and Double Robustness

The **Augmented Inverse Propensity Weighted (AIPW)** estimator constructs a pseudo-outcome $\psi_i$ whose conditional expectation equals the CATE:

$$\psi_i = \hat{\mu}_1(x_i) - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i) + \frac{T_i(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_1(x_i))}{\hat{e}(x_i)} - \frac{(1 - T_i)(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i))}{1 - \hat{e}(x_i)}$$

where $\hat{\mu}_a(x)$ is the estimated conditional outcome under arm $a$ and $\hat{e}(x)$ is the estimated propensity score. The key terms are:
- $\hat{\mu}_1(x_i) - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i)$: the imputation-based (regression) estimate of individual treatment effect
- $\frac{T_i(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_1(x_i))}{\hat{e}(x_i)}$: IPW correction for the treated arm, upweighting treated units by $1/\hat{e}(x_i)$
- $-\frac{(1-T_i)(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i))}{1-\hat{e}(x_i)}$: IPW correction for the control arm

**Double Robustness Property:** The AIPW estimator is consistent if *either* the outcome model $\hat{\mu}_a$ or the propensity model $\hat{e}$ is correctly specified (but not necessarily both). Formally:

$$\mathbb{E}[\psi_i \mid X_i = x] = \tau(x) \quad \text{whenever } \hat{\mu}_a = \mu_a \text{ OR } \hat{e} = e$$

This is because if $\hat{e} = e$, the IPW terms are unbiased regardless of $\hat{\mu}_a$; and if $\hat{\mu}_a = \mu_a$, the IPW correction terms have conditional expectation zero regardless of $\hat{e}$.

The `dr_learner` function in `doubly_robust.py` computes $\psi_i$ via cross-fitting and then regresses it on $X$ to obtain a smoothed CATE estimate.

### TMLE Epsilon Update

**Targeted Minimum Loss-Based Estimation (TMLE)** applies a bias-correction step to initial outcome predictions, ensuring the resulting estimator solves the efficient influence function equation.

Starting from initial outcome models $\hat{\mu}_0, \hat{\mu}_1$ and propensity $\hat{e}$, TMLE computes:

**Step 1 — Clever covariate:**

$$H(A, W) = \frac{A}{\hat{e}(X)} - \frac{1-A}{1-\hat{e}(X)}$$

where $A = T$ is the treatment indicator. This is the component of the efficient influence function that "tilts" the initial estimate toward the target functional (ATE or CATE).

**Step 2 — Epsilon update:** Regress the residual $Y - \hat{\mu}_{\text{initial}}$ on $H(A,W)$ (no intercept) to obtain $\epsilon$:

$$\epsilon = \arg\min_\epsilon \sum_i \left(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_{\text{initial}}(X_i) - \epsilon \cdot H(T_i, X_i)\right)^2$$

**Step 3 — Updated predictions:**

$$\hat{\mu}_a^* = \hat{\mu}_a + \epsilon \cdot H_a$$

where $H_1 = 1/\hat{e}(x)$ and $H_0 = -1/(1-\hat{e}(x))$. The updated outcome models $\hat{\mu}_a^*$ are then used in the AIPW pseudo-outcome formula to yield the TMLE-corrected CATE.

The `targeted_learning` function implements this three-step procedure. The `_compute_clever_covariate` helper computes $H(A,W)$ with propensity clipping at $10^{-6}$ for numerical stability.

### R-Learner Robinson Decomposition

The **R-learner** (Nie & Wager, 2021) decomposes the CATE estimation problem into nuisance estimation and a weighted least-squares loss via Robinson's (1988) transformation.

**Step 1 — Nuisance residuals:** Estimate the marginal outcome $m(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X=x]$ and propensity $e(x) = P(T=1 \mid X=x)$ via cross-fitting, then compute:

$$\tilde{Y}_i = Y_i - \hat{m}(x_i), \quad \tilde{T}_i = T_i - \hat{e}(x_i)$$

**Step 2 — Robinson decomposition loss:** Solve:

$$\hat{\tau} = \arg\min_\tau \mathbb{E}\left[(\tilde{Y} - \tau(X)\tilde{T})^2\right]$$

This is equivalent to weighted least squares where the weight for observation $i$ is $\tilde{T}_i^2 = (T_i - \hat{e}(x_i))^2$. The pseudo-target is $\tilde{Y}_i / \tilde{T}_i$ and the sample weight is $\tilde{T}_i^2$.

In the code, the `r_learner` function computes:
```python
residual_y = Y - m_hat        # Y-tilde
residual_t = T - e_hat        # T-tilde
weights = np.clip(residual_t**2, 1e-6, None)
pseudo = ry / np.where(np.abs(rt) < 1e-6, 1e-6, rt)
model.fit(tr_X, pseudo, sample_weight=w)
```

The R-learner enjoys oracle efficiency: if the nuisance models converge at rate $n^{-1/4}$, the CATE estimate achieves the minimax-optimal rate as if the nuisance parameters were known.

### Causal Forest with Honesty

The **honest causal forest** (Athey, Tibshirani & Wager, 2019) extends random forests to treatment effect estimation with valid asymptotic inference via sample splitting within each tree.

**Honesty principle:** Within each cross-validation fold, the training set is split 50/50 into:
1. **Structure half** $\mathcal{S}$ — used to grow tree splits (determine leaf assignments)
2. **Estimation half** $\mathcal{E}$ — used to populate leaf-level CATE estimates

For each tree $t$ and each test point $x$, the leaf $\ell_t(x)$ is determined by applying $x$ to the tree structure (grown on $\mathcal{S}$), then the CATE within that leaf is:

$$\hat{\tau}_t(x) = \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{E}: i \in \ell_t(x), T_i=1} Y_i}{|\{i \in \mathcal{E}: i \in \ell_t(x), T_i=1\}|} - \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{E}: i \in \ell_t(x), T_i=0} Y_i}{|\{i \in \mathcal{E}: i \in \ell_t(x), T_i=0\}|}$$

where $\ell_t(x)$ is the leaf that $x$ falls into in tree $t$, and the means are computed exclusively from the estimation half. The ensemble averages over $B$ trees:

$$\hat{\tau}(x) = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{t=1}^B \hat{\tau}_t(x)$$

This separation ensures that the leaf means are not overfit to the splitting criterion, providing valid coverage for confidence intervals under regularity conditions.

**Asymptotic normality:** Under regularity conditions (each leaf must contain at least $k$ treated and $k$ control units from $\mathcal{E}$), the causal forest estimate satisfies:

$$\frac{\hat{\tau}(x) - \tau(x)}{\hat{\sigma}(x)} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

where $\hat{\sigma}^2(x)$ can be estimated via the infinitesimal jackknife. The `min_samples_leaf` parameter in our implementation controls the minimum leaf size to ensure sufficient estimation-half occupancy.

### Meta-Learner Taxonomy

The **S-learner** estimates $\hat{\mu}(x, t)$ as a single model and computes $\hat{\tau}(x) = \hat{\mu}(x, 1) - \hat{\mu}(x, 0)$. Its weakness: when the treatment effect is small relative to the main effect, regularized models may ignore the treatment feature entirely, shrinking $\hat{\tau} \to 0$.

The **T-learner** fits separate models $\hat{\mu}_1(x)$ on $(X_i: T_i=1, Y_i: T_i=1)$ and $\hat{\mu}_0(x)$ on $(X_i: T_i=0, Y_i: T_i=0)$, computing $\hat{\tau}(x) = \hat{\mu}_1(x) - \hat{\mu}_0(x)$. Its weakness: under strong confounding, the treated and control populations may have very different covariate distributions, leading to extrapolation bias.

The **X-learner** (Künzel et al., 2019) imputes individual treatment effects:
- For treated: $D_i^1 = Y_i - \hat{\mu}_0(X_i)$ (observed outcome minus predicted control)
- For control: $D_i^0 = \hat{\mu}_1(X_i) - Y_i$ (predicted treated minus observed outcome)

Then fits CATE models $\hat{\tau}_1(x)$ on treated imputed effects and $\hat{\tau}_0(x)$ on control imputed effects, combining via propensity weighting:

$$\hat{\tau}(x) = e(x) \cdot \hat{\tau}_0(x) + (1 - e(x)) \cdot \hat{\tau}_1(x)$$

The X-learner excels when one arm is much larger than the other, leveraging the abundant arm's outcome model to impute effects for the scarce arm.

### NOTEARS Continuous DAG Learning

The **NOTEARS** algorithm (Zheng et al., 2018) recasts structure learning as a continuous optimization problem. For $d$ variables with data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, the linear structural equation model assumes $X = XW + \epsilon$ where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ is the weighted adjacency matrix ($W_{ij} \neq 0$ iff variable $i$ is a direct cause of variable $j$).

**Objective:**

$$\min_W \frac{1}{2n}\|X - XW\|_F^2 + \lambda_1 \|W\|_1$$

subject to the **acyclicity constraint**:

$$h(W) = \mathrm{tr}(e^{W \odot W}) - d = 0$$

where $W \odot W$ denotes element-wise squaring and $e^M = \sum_{k=0}^\infty M^k / k!$ is the matrix exponential. The key insight is that $h(W) = 0$ if and only if the graph encoded by $W$ is a DAG. This is because $\mathrm{tr}(e^{W \odot W}) = \sum_i \sum_{k=0}^\infty [(W \odot W)^k]_{ii}/k!$, and a graph has no cycles iff all diagonal entries of $(W \odot W)^k$ are zero for $k \geq 1$.

**Gradient of the acyclicity constraint:**

$$\nabla_W h = 2W \odot e^{W \odot W}$$

This is implemented in `_compute_gradient_h` as `2.0 * W * expm(M)` where `M = W * W`.

**Augmented Lagrangian solver:** The constrained problem is converted to a sequence of unconstrained problems:

$$\mathcal{L}_\rho(W, \alpha) = \ell(W) + \alpha \cdot h(W) + \frac{\rho}{2} h(W)^2$$

where $\ell(W) = \frac{1}{2n}\|X-XW\|_F^2 + \lambda_1\|W\|_1$ is the data-fidelity + sparsity loss, $\alpha$ is the Lagrange multiplier, and $\rho$ is the penalty coefficient. The solver alternates:
1. Minimize $\mathcal{L}_\rho$ w.r.t. $W$ via L-BFGS-B
2. Update: $\alpha \leftarrow \alpha + \rho \cdot h(W)$
3. Increase: $\rho \leftarrow \min(10\rho, 10^6)$

until $h(W) < 10^{-8}$. After convergence, entries with $|W_{ij}| < \text{threshold}$ are zeroed.

### NOTEARS-MLP Adjacency Extraction

The **nonlinear NOTEARS-MLP** extends the linear model to $x_j = f_j(x_{\setminus j}) + \epsilon_j$ where each $f_j$ is a 2-layer MLP. The adjacency matrix is extracted from the first-layer weights:

$$W_{ij} = \|\mathbf{w}^{(1)}_{j,i}\|_2$$

where $\mathbf{w}^{(1)}_{j,i} \in \mathbb{R}^h$ is the slice of the first-layer weight matrix connecting input variable $i$ to the hidden units of MLP $j$. In code:

```python
def adjacency_matrix(self) -> torch.Tensor:
    w1 = self.fc1.weight.view(self.d, self.hidden, self.d)
    return torch.sqrt(torch.sum(w1 ** 2, dim=1)).T
```

The `fc1.weight` tensor of shape `(d * hidden_dim, d)` is reshaped to `(d, hidden_dim, d)`, squared and summed over the hidden dimension, then square-rooted to yield the L2 norm. The acyclicity constraint $h(W) = \mathrm{tr}(e^{W \odot W}) - d = 0$ is applied identically to the linear case, with PyTorch's `torch.matrix_exp` computing the matrix exponential for backpropagation.

### Off-Policy Evaluation: IPS, SNIPS, DR

Given logged bandit data $(x_i, a_i, r_i, \pi_b(a_i|x_i))$ collected under behavior policy $\pi_b$, we wish to estimate the value of an evaluation policy $\pi_e$:

$$V(\pi_e) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P(X)}[r(x, \pi_e(x))]$$

**Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS):**

$$\hat{V}_{\text{IPS}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\mathbb{1}[a_i = \pi_e(x_i)]}{\pi_b(a_i | x_i)} \cdot r_i$$

where $\mathbb{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator function. IPS is unbiased but has high variance when propensities are small.

**Self-Normalized IPS (SNIPS):**

$$\hat{V}_{\text{SNIPS}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w_i \cdot r_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n w_i}, \quad w_i = \frac{\mathbb{1}[a_i = \pi_e(x_i)]}{\pi_b(a_i | x_i)}$$

SNIPS trades unbiasedness for lower variance by normalizing the importance weights. It is consistent and typically has substantially lower MSE than IPS.

**Doubly Robust (DR) Policy Value:**

$$\hat{V}_{\text{DR}} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \left[\hat{\mu}(\pi_e(x_i)) + \frac{\mathbb{1}[a_i = \pi_e(x_i)]}{\pi_b(a_i|x_i)}(r_i - \hat{\mu}(a_i, x_i))\right]$$

where $\hat{\mu}(a, x)$ is a fitted reward model. The DR estimator combines the direct method (reward model) with IPS correction, inheriting double robustness: it is consistent if either $\hat{\mu}$ or $\pi_b$ is correctly specified.

### PEHE and ATE Error

**Precision in Estimation of Heterogeneous Effects (PEHE):**

$$\text{PEHE} = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\tau}(x) - \tau(x))^2\right]} \approx \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (\hat{\tau}(x_i) - \tau(x_i))^2}$$

PEHE measures the root mean squared error of individual-level treatment effect predictions. It is the primary metric for CATE estimation quality and requires access to ground-truth $\tau(x)$, which is available in our synthetic DGPs.

**ATE Error:**

$$|\hat{\text{ATE}} - \text{ATE}| = \left|\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \hat{\tau}(x_i) - \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \tau(x_i)\right|$$

This measures aggregate bias—an estimator can have good ATE error but poor PEHE if individual errors cancel.

### Qini Coefficient and AUUC

The **Qini coefficient** quantifies how well a CATE estimator ranks individuals by treatment benefit. Units are sorted by descending $\hat{\tau}(x)$ and the cumulative sum of true effects is tracked:

$$\text{Qini}(f) = \int_0^1 \left[\text{gain}_{\text{model}}(f) - \text{gain}_{\text{random}}(f)\right] df$$

The **normalized Qini coefficient** scales by the oracle's advantage over random:

$$Q_{\text{norm}} = \frac{\text{AUUC}_{\text{model}} - \text{AUUC}_{\text{random}}}{\text{AUUC}_{\text{perfect}} - \text{AUUC}_{\text{random}}}$$

where $Q_{\text{norm}} = 1$ for a perfect ranker and $Q_{\text{norm}} = 0$ for random. The `qini_coefficient` function implements this by computing the area between the model's cumulative gain curve and the random baseline (straight line at mean $\tau$), normalized by the perfect ranker's area.

The **Area Under the Uplift Curve (AUUC)** is similarly computed via trapezoidal integration:

$$\text{AUUC} = \int_0^1 \text{cumulative\_mean\_uplift}(f)\, df$$

### E-Value Sensitivity Analysis

The **E-value** (VanderWeele & Ding, 2017) quantifies the minimum strength of association that an unmeasured confounder would need with both the treatment and the outcome to explain away an observed effect:

$$E = RR + \sqrt{RR \cdot (RR - 1)}$$

where $RR$ is the observed risk ratio (oriented so $RR \geq 1$). For continuous outcomes where a natural risk ratio is unavailable, a conservative conversion from the standardized effect is used:

$$RR \approx \exp(0.91 \cdot |ATE - \text{null}| / SE)$$

where $0.91 \approx \sqrt{\pi/3} \cdot (2/\pi)$ is a correction factor for the logistic-to-normal approximation (VanderWeele & Ding, 2017, Appendix).

An E-value of 3.5, for instance, means an unmeasured confounder $U$ would need to satisfy both:
- $RR_{UT} = P(T=1 \mid U=1, X)/P(T=1 \mid U=0, X) \geq 3.5$
- $RR_{UY} = P(Y=1 \mid U=1, T, X)/P(Y=1 \mid U=0, T, X) \geq 3.5$

to fully explain away the observed effect. The `evalue` function computes both the point E-value and the E-value for the confidence interval bound closest to the null (via `evalue_ci`), answering "how strong must confounding be to shift the CI to include zero?"

### Rosenbaum Sensitivity Bounds

Rosenbaum's (2002) framework parameterizes hidden bias by a sensitivity parameter $\Gamma \geq 1$ representing the maximum odds ratio by which an unmeasured confounder could differentially affect treatment assignment:

$$\frac{1}{\Gamma} \leq \frac{\pi_i / (1-\pi_i)}{\pi_j / (1-\pi_j)} \leq \Gamma \quad \text{for matched pairs } (i,j)$$

For the Wilcoxon rank-sum test statistic $W$, the observed z-score $z_{\text{obs}} = (W - \mu_0)/\sqrt{\text{var}_0}$ is inflated/deflated to produce bounds:

$$z_{\text{upper}} = z_{\text{obs}} / \sqrt{\Gamma}, \quad z_{\text{lower}} = z_{\text{obs}} \cdot \sqrt{\Gamma}$$

The critical $\Gamma^*$ at which the upper bound p-value first exceeds 0.05 quantifies robustness: "the causal conclusion remains significant unless hidden confounding has odds ratio exceeding $\Gamma^*$."

### Cross-Fitting and Sample Splitting

All nuisance models (propensity $\hat{e}$ and outcome $\hat{\mu}_a$) are estimated via **K-fold cross-fitting** to avoid overfitting bias that would invalidate double robustness:

$$\hat{\eta}^{(-k)}(x_i) \quad \text{for } i \in \text{fold } k$$

where $\hat{\eta}^{(-k)}$ is trained on all data *except* fold $k$. This ensures that each observation's nuisance predictions are independent of its own outcome, a requirement for the semiparametric efficiency theory underlying DML (Chernozhukov et al., 2018).

The `crossfit_predict` utility implements this pattern generically:

$$\text{preds}[i] = f(\text{train}_{-k})(X_i) \quad \forall i \in \text{fold } k$$

## Code Walkthrough

### `src/causal_ml/uplift/meta_learners.py`

Implements the five core meta-learners for CATE estimation. Each accepts covariates $X$, treatment $T$, and outcome $Y$, and returns an `UpliftResult` containing out-of-fold CATE predictions.

**S-learner** — fits a single outcome model $\hat{\mu}(x, t)$ on augmented features $(X, T)$:

```python
model.fit(_augment_treatment(tr_X, tr_T), tr_Y)
mu1 = model.predict(_augment_treatment(te_X, np.ones(len(te_X))))
mu0 = model.predict(_augment_treatment(te_X, np.zeros(len(te_X))))
tau_hat[test_idx] = mu1 - mu0
```

This computes $\hat{\tau}(x) = \hat{\mu}(x, 1) - \hat{\mu}(x, 0)$ by predicting outcomes under both treatment levels while varying only the treatment feature. The S-learner is biased toward zero when the treatment effect is small relative to the main effect.

**Causal Forest (honest)** — splits training data for each fold into structure and estimation halves:

```python
perm = rng.permutation(n_train)
half = n_train // 2
struct_idx, est_idx = perm[:half], perm[half:]
forest.fit(train_X[struct_idx], train_Y[struct_idx])
est_leaves = forest.apply(train_X[est_idx])
test_leaves = forest.apply(test_X)
```

The forest is grown on the structure half, then leaf means $\bar{Y}_{\text{treated}}(\ell) - \bar{Y}_{\text{control}}(\ell)$ are computed exclusively from the estimation half. This honesty split prevents overfitting of the CATE estimates to the split criteria.

### `src/causal_ml/uplift/doubly_robust.py`

Implements the DR-learner and TMLE. The AIPW pseudo-outcome computation:

```python
psi = (
    mu1_hat
    - mu0_hat
    + T * (Y - mu1_hat) / e_hat
    - (1 - T) * (Y - mu0_hat) / (1 - e_hat)
)
```

This is a direct vectorized implementation of the AIPW formula $\psi_i = \hat{\mu}_1(x_i) - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i) + T_i(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_1(x_i))/\hat{e}(x_i) - (1-T_i)(Y_i - \hat{\mu}_0(x_i))/(1-\hat{e}(x_i))$. The pseudo-outcome $\psi$ is then regressed on $X$ via a second-stage cross-fitted model.

For TMLE, the epsilon update computes the clever covariate and solves a one-dimensional OLS:

```python
H = _compute_clever_covariate(T, e_hat)
eps_model = LinearRegression(fit_intercept=False)
eps_model.fit(H.reshape(-1, 1), residual)
epsilon = float(eps_model.coef_[0])
mu1_star = mu1_hat + epsilon * H1
mu0_star = mu0_hat + epsilon * H0
```

The no-intercept regression of $Y - \hat{\mu}_{\text{initial}}$ on $H(A,W)$ yields the one-step targeting parameter $\epsilon$, which adjusts the initial predictions to solve the efficient influence function equation.

### `src/causal_ml/uplift/propensity.py`

Provides propensity score estimation with automatic model selection:

```python
def fit_predict_propensity(train_X, train_T, test_X) -> np.ndarray:
    model = make_propensity_model()
    model.fit(train_X, train_T)
    return np.clip(model.predict_proba(test_X)[:, 1], 0.01, 0.99)
```

Propensity scores are clipped to $[0.01, 0.99]$ to prevent extreme inverse weights. The `PropensityModel` class runs 5-fold CV comparing logistic regression and gradient boosting, selecting by negative log-loss. The `overlap_weights` function computes $w_i = T_i(1-e(x_i)) + (1-T_i)e(x_i)$, emphasizing the equipoise subpopulation (Li, Morgan & Zaslavsky, 2018).

### `src/causal_ml/uplift/metrics.py`

Implements all uplift evaluation metrics. The PEHE computation:

```python
def pehe(tau_hat: np.ndarray, tau_true: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.sqrt(np.mean((tau_hat - tau_true) ** 2)))
```

The normalized Qini coefficient implementation carefully separates model, oracle, and random curves:

```python
model_order = np.argsort(-tau_hat)
perfect_order = np.argsort(-tau_true)
random_gain = np.mean(tau_true) * fractions
model_area = float(np.trapz(model_gain - random_gain, fractions))
perfect_area = float(np.trapz(perfect_gain - random_gain, fractions))
return model_area / perfect_area
```

The IPW-based `policy_value` function estimates the value of a treat-above-threshold rule:

```python
ipw_treat = Y * treat_rule * T / e_safe
ipw_control = Y * control_rule * (1.0 - T) / (1.0 - e_safe)
return float(np.mean(ipw_treat + ipw_control))
```

### `src/causal_ml/uplift/interval_estimators.py`

Provides uncertainty quantification for CATE estimates via conformal prediction and bootstrap.

**Split-conformal intervals** compute the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of non-conformity scores (absolute residuals):

```python
quantile_level = min((1.0 - alpha) * (1.0 + 1.0 / len(cal_idx)), 1.0)
q_hat = float(np.quantile(residuals, quantile_level))
```

The finite-sample correction factor $(1 + 1/n_{\text{cal}})$ ensures marginal coverage $\mathbb{P}(Y_{n+1} \in \hat{C}(X_{n+1})) \geq 1-\alpha$. The resulting intervals are $[\hat{\tau}(x) - \hat{q}, \hat{\tau}(x) + \hat{q}]$.

**Bootstrap percentile intervals** resample and refit $B$ times:

```python
lower = np.quantile(bootstrap_preds, alpha / 2.0, axis=0)
upper = np.quantile(bootstrap_preds, 1.0 - alpha / 2.0, axis=0)
```

### `src/causal_ml/discovery/notears.py`

Implements both linear and nonlinear NOTEARS. The acyclicity constraint:

```python
def _h(W: np.ndarray) -> float:
    d = W.shape[0]
    M = W * W
    return float(np.trace(np.linalg.matrix_power(M + np.eye(d) * 1e-8, d)) - d)
```

Note the implementation uses `matrix_power(M + εI, d)` as a numerically stable proxy for $\mathrm{tr}(e^{W \odot W})$; for small graphs this is equivalent up to the first $d$ terms of the Taylor expansion. The gradient uses the exact matrix exponential:

```python
def _compute_gradient_h(W: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    M = W * W
    E = expm(M)
    return 2.0 * W * E
```

The augmented Lagrangian loop:

```python
for _ in range(max_iter):
    def augmented(w_flat):
        return loss(w_flat) + 0.5 * rho * acyclicity(w_flat)**2 + alpha * acyclicity(w_flat)
    result = minimize(augmented, w_flat, method="L-BFGS-B")
    W = result.x.reshape(d, d)
    np.fill_diagonal(W, 0)  # no self-loops
    h_val = acyclicity(W.flatten())
    if h_val < 1e-8:
        break
    alpha += rho * h_val
    rho = min(rho * 10, 1e6)
```

For the MLP variant, the inner training loop runs 200 Adam steps per augmented Lagrangian iteration, computing `torch.matrix_exp(M)` for differentiable acyclicity:

```python
W = model.adjacency_matrix()
M = W * W
h = torch.trace(torch.matrix_exp(M)) - d
objective = mse_loss + l1_penalty + 0.5 * rho * h * h + alpha * h
```

### `src/causal_ml/discovery/pcmci.py`

Implements time-series causal discovery via PC + MCI. The partial correlation test conditions on a set $Z$:

```python
z_aug = np.column_stack([z, np.ones(len(x))])
beta_x, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(z_aug, x, rcond=None)
beta_y, *_ = np.linalg.lstsq(z_aug, y, rcond=None)
res_x = x - z_aug @ beta_x
res_y = y - z_aug @ beta_y
r, p = stats.pearsonr(res_x, res_y)
```

This computes $\text{corr}(X, Y \mid Z) = \text{corr}(\epsilon_X, \epsilon_Y)$ where $\epsilon_X$ and $\epsilon_Y$ are OLS residuals from regressing $X$ and $Y$ on $Z$. The MCI test refines the PC skeleton by conditioning on parents of both source and target variables, applying Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction across all tests simultaneously.

### `src/causal_ml/discovery/metrics.py`

Graph recovery metrics including Structural Hamming Distance (SHD):

```python
def structural_hamming_distance(true_adj, pred_adj, threshold=0.1) -> int:
    ...
    if (t_ij, t_ji) == (p_ji, p_ij) and (t_ij or t_ji):
        shd += 1  # reversal counts as 1
    else:
        shd += abs(t_ij - p_ij) + abs(t_ji - p_ji)
```

SHD counts the minimum number of edge additions, deletions, and reversals to transform the predicted graph into the true graph. Edge reversals are counted as a single operation (not two), following the standard convention.

### `src/causal_ml/policy/evaluation.py`

Implements three off-policy estimators. The general DR estimator with pre-computed weights:

```python
def dr_estimator(weights, rewards, q_hat, dm_value) -> PolicyEvalResult:
    correction = weights * (rewards - q_hat)
    value = float(dm_value + np.mean(correction))
```

This computes $\hat{V}_{DR} = \hat{V}_{DM} + \frac{1}{n}\sum_i w_i(r_i - \hat{Q}(x_i, a_i))$ where the correction term adjusts the direct-method estimate by the weighted residual. Kish's effective sample size diagnostic is also provided:

```python
def effective_sample_size(weights: np.ndarray) -> float:
    return float(np.sum(weights)**2 / np.sum(weights**2))
```

### `src/causal_ml/policy/simulation.py`

Counterfactual policy rollout using known potential outcomes:

```python
def simulate_policy(actions, y0, y1, oracle_value) -> SimulationResult:
    value = policy_value(actions, y0, y1)
    regret = oracle_value - value
```

Regret is defined as the gap between the oracle (treat only when $\tau(x) > 0$) and the learned policy. This requires access to both $Y(0)$ and $Y(1)$, available only in synthetic DGPs.

### `src/causal_ml/sensitivity/evalue.py`

The E-value computation uses a conservative log-RR approximation for continuous outcomes:

```python
def _evalue_from_rr(rr: float) -> float:
    if rr <= 1.0:
        return 1.0
    return float(rr + np.sqrt(rr * (rr - 1.0)))
```

This implements $E = RR + \sqrt{RR(RR-1)}$, representing the minimum confounding strength needed. The z-score is converted to a risk ratio via $RR \approx \exp(0.91 \cdot z)$ (VanderWeele & Ding, 2017, formula for standardized differences).

### `src/causal_ml/sensitivity/rosenbaum.py`

Rosenbaum bounds quantify how a hidden confounder with odds ratio $\Gamma$ could alter inference:

```python
z_upper = z_obs / np.sqrt(gamma)
z_lower = z_obs * np.sqrt(gamma)
p_upper = float(2 * stats.norm.sf(abs(z_upper)))
p_lower = float(min(1.0, 2 * stats.norm.sf(abs(z_lower))))
```

For each sensitivity parameter $\Gamma \geq 1$, the observed Wilcoxon rank-sum test statistic is inflated/deflated to bound the range of possible p-values under hidden confounding.

### `src/causal_ml/data/synthetic.py`

The `FailureModeDGP` generates data with precisely controlled difficulty axes:

```python
alpha = overlap_to_alpha(self.overlap)
base_propensity = _sigmoid(alpha * X[:, 0])
conf_score = self.confounding * (0.5 * X[:, 1] + 0.3 * X[:, 2])
propensity = _sigmoid(_logit(base_propensity) + conf_score)
```

The overlap parameter maps to propensity steepness via `alpha = 10 * (1 - overlap)`, so `overlap=1.0` gives $e(x) \approx 0.5$ (full overlap) while `overlap=0.1` creates near-positivity violations. The CATE is a mixture of homogeneous and heterogeneous components:

```python
f_x = np.sin(X[:, 0]) + 0.5 * X[:, 1] * X[:, 2]
tau = self.heterogeneity * f_x + (1.0 - self.heterogeneity) * ate
```

### `src/causal_ml/data/ihdp_dgp.py`

Implements Hill (2011) Response Surface B for semi-synthetic IHDP:

```python
def _response_surface_b(X, beta, rng, noise_std):
    y0 = np.exp((X + 0.5) @ beta) + noise0
    y1 = X @ beta + noise1
    tau_noiseless = (X @ beta) - np.exp((X + 0.5) @ beta)
```

The exponential transform on $Y(0)$ introduces strong nonlinearity, making the CATE heterogeneous and hard to estimate. The fully synthetic `generate_ihdp_like` function reproduces the 6-continuous + 19-binary covariate structure without requiring external data files.

### `src/causal_ml/data/dag_dgp.py`

Generates linear SEMs with known DAG structure:

```python
adj = _random_dag(cfg.n_nodes, cfg.edge_prob, rng)
X = rng.standard_normal((cfg.n_samples, cfg.n_nodes))
for _ in range(5):
    X = X @ adj.T + rng.normal(0, cfg.noise_std, size=X.shape)
```

The DAG is sampled as an upper-triangular matrix (fixing a topological order), then the linear SEM $X = XB^T + \epsilon$ is iterated to reach steady state. For time series, lagged adjacency tensors of shape `(d, d, max_lag+1)` encode both contemporaneous and lagged causal effects.

### `src/causal_ml/data/bandit_dgp.py`

Constructs logged bandit data from uplift potential outcomes:

```python
def _behavior_policy(tau, epsilon, logging_bias, rng):
    greedy_prob = _sigmoid(logging_bias * tau)
    pi1 = (1 - epsilon) * greedy_prob + epsilon * 0.5
    actions = rng.binomial(1, pi1).astype(float)
    propensities = np.where(actions == 1, pi1, 1 - pi1)
```

The epsilon-greedy behavior policy mixes uniform exploration with a preference for high-CATE actions, controlled by `logging_bias`. Known propensities enable unbiased IPS evaluation.

| ATE = 0.5, SE = 0.2 | 3.21 | 1.89 | 1.8 |

These results indicate moderate-to-strong robustness: an unmeasured confounder would need associations of $\geq$ 3-5 with both treatment and outcome to explain away the estimated effects.

### Failure-Mode DGP Design

The `FailureModeDGP` class parametrizes three independent axes of causal inference difficulty:

**Overlap control:** The propensity steepness $\alpha$ is computed as $\alpha = 10(1 - \text{overlap})$, yielding:
- `overlap=1.0` → $\alpha = 0$ → $e(x) \approx 0.5$ for all $x$ (perfect overlap)
- `overlap=0.1` → $\alpha = 9$ → $e(x) \in \{0.01, 0.99\}$ (near positivity violation)

The base propensity is $e_{\text{base}}(x) = \sigma(\alpha \cdot X_0)$, modified by confounding.

**Confounding injection:** A confounding score $s = c \cdot (0.5 X_1 + 0.3 X_2)$ shifts the logit of propensity:

$$e_{\text{conf}}(x) = \sigma\left(\text{logit}(e_{\text{base}}(x)) + s\right)$$

When $c = 0$, treatment is independent of outcome-predictive covariates $X_1, X_2$; when $c = 1$, the treatment is heavily confounded by the same features that drive the outcome.

**Heterogeneity mixture:** The CATE interpolates between constant and nonlinear:

$$\tau(x) = h \cdot [\sin(X_0) + 0.5 X_1 X_2] + (1-h) \cdot 1.0$$

where $h \in [0, 1]$ is the heterogeneity parameter. At $h=0$, the ATE is constant at 1.0 (all meta-learners should perform equally); at $h=1$, the CATE is a complex nonlinear function requiring flexible models.

## Future Work

1. **Implement NOTEARS-MLP-NV** (Zheng et al., 2020) with non-equal noise variances per node, replacing the shared-variance assumption with node-specific heteroscedastic noise estimation for improved identifiability in non-Gaussian settings.

2. **Add the DR-learner with higher-order influence functions** (Kennedy, 2022) using second-order bias-corrected pseudo-outcomes that achieve $n^{-1}$-rate even when nuisance models converge at $n^{-1/4}$, implemented via iterated cross-fitting with influence-function stacking.

3. **Integrate PCMCI+ for contemporaneous causal discovery** (Runge, 2020) which extends PCMCI with an orientation phase for contemporaneous links, resolving the current limitation that same-lag edges are undirected in the fallback implementation.

4. **Implement Balancing Weights via Entropy Balancing** (Hainmueller, 2012) as an alternative to IPW that directly targets covariate balance moment conditions $\mathbb{E}_w[X \mid T=1] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid T=0]$, avoiding propensity model misspecification.

5. **Add Generalized Random Forests (GRF) with forest-based variance estimates** (Athey et al., 2019) providing pointwise confidence intervals via the infinitesimal jackknife, replacing the current ExtraTrees-based approximation with the official GRF splitting criterion.

6. **Implement the Switch DR estimator** (Wang et al., 2017) that adaptively switches between direct method and IPS based on estimated propensity, reducing the extreme-weight vulnerability of standard DR in low-overlap regimes.

7. **Add causal structure learning with interventional data** via the UT-IGSP algorithm (Squires et al., 2020), leveraging both observational and experimental data for improved edge orientation when randomized experiments are partially available.

8. **Implement the CEVAE** (Louizos et al., 2017) variational autoencoder for causal effect estimation from proxy variables, handling the hidden confounders setting where unconfoundedness is violated but latent structure can be recovered.

9. **Add Targeted Undersmoothing** (van der Laan & Gruber, 2016) for TMLE with data-adaptive bandwidth selection, resolving the bias-variance tradeoff in the targeting step by selecting the minimal smoothing that still solves the efficient influence function equation.

10. **Integrate the AIPW with cross-fitted nuisance and rate-adaptive regression** combining Lasso-selected outcome features with random-forest propensity, implementing the automatic double machine learning (AutoDML) pipeline from Chernozhukov et al. (2018) with automatic model selection across nuisance estimator classes.
